Verb Tense — Presentation Script

English Class · Full bilingual script (English + Spanish)

Slide 1 Introduction — Verb Tense

Hello everyone! Today I'm going to be talking about verb tenses in English. Verb tenses are one of the most important aspects of the English language because they allow us to communicate when an action happens — whether it took place in the past, is happening right now, or will happen in the future.

In this presentation, we are going to focus on three main groups: the Past Simple, the Past Continuous, and the Future. For each one, I'll explain what it is, when to use it, how to form it, and give you some examples. Let's get started!

Slide 2 Past Simple — Overview

The first tense we'll look at is the Past Simple. As the name suggests, it is used to talk about actions that were completed in the past — actions that started and finished at a specific time before now.

There are three main situations where we use the Past Simple. First, for completed actions — things that happened and are done, like "I ate breakfast at seven o'clock." Second, for past habits — things we used to do regularly, such as "She walked to school every day when she was a child." And third, for past events in a sequence — when we tell a story and actions happen one after another, like "He woke up, took a shower, and left the house."

Slide 3 Past Simple — Auxiliary Verb: DID

Now, let's talk about the auxiliary verb for the Past Simple. The auxiliary is DID, which comes from the verb "to do." It's important to know that DID is only used in negative and interrogative sentences — not in affirmative ones.

In negative sentences, we say "did not", or in its contracted form, "didn't". For example: "She didn't go to the party." In questions, DID goes at the beginning of the sentence: "Did you call your mom?"

One important rule to remember is that when you use DID or DIDN'T, the main verb stays in its base form — it does NOT change. You never say "He didn't went" — the correct form is "He didn't go."

💡 Tip: DID already carries the past meaning, so the main verb doesn't need to change.
Slide 4–5 Past Simple — Structure & Formation

Let's look at how to form the Past Simple. For affirmative sentences, the structure is: Subject + Verb in Past Tense + Object.

Now, here's an important distinction: verbs in English are either regular or irregular. Regular verbs simply add -ed at the end: walk → walked, talk → talked, play → played, work → worked, visit → visited.

Irregular verbs, on the other hand, change completely and you have to memorize them: go → went, eat → ate, see → saw, buy → bought, write → wrote.

For negative sentences, the structure is: Subject + did not (didn't) + base verb + Object. Example: "Jim did not finish his homework."

For questions, the structure is: DID + Subject + base verb + Object. Example: "Did you listen to music last night?"

💡 Remember: in negatives and questions, the main verb ALWAYS goes back to its base form because DID already marks the past.
Slide 6 Past Continuous — Overview

Now let's move on to the Past Continuous, also called the Past Progressive. While the Past Simple describes a completed action, the Past Continuous describes an action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past — it was already happening and continued for a period of time.

We use it in three key situations. First, for actions that were in progress at a specific past moment — something was happening at a particular time. Second, for interrupted actions — when one action was in progress and another action suddenly interrupted it. Third, for parallel actions — two actions happening at the same time, usually connected with the word "while."

We also use it to set the background scene in a story — to describe what was happening when the main event occurred.

Slide 7 Past Continuous — Auxiliary: WAS / WERE

The auxiliary verbs for the Past Continuous are WAS and WERE, which are the past forms of the verb "to be."

The choice between WAS and WERE depends on the subject: use WAS with singular subjects — I, he, she, it. Use WERE with plural subjects — you, we, they.

In negative sentences, we use the contracted forms: wasn't (was not) and weren't (were not). For example: "He wasn't sleeping" or "They weren't paying attention."

💡 Trick to remember: WAS = singular (1 person), WERE = plural (more than 1) — and also "you," even when referring to one person.
Slide 8–9 Past Continuous — Structure & Examples

The structure for the affirmative is: Subject + was/were + verb (-ing). The main verb always takes the -ing ending. Examples: "She was studying at 9 pm last night." — "He was cooking while she was watching TV." — "They were sleeping when the phone rang."

For negatives: Subject + was/were + not + verb(-ing). Example: "She was not studying." or "She wasn't studying."

For questions: Was/Were + Subject + verb(-ing)? Example: "Was she studying?" or "Were they listening?"

A very common pattern in English is combining Past Continuous with Past Simple using WHEN or WHILE: "I was reading when the lights went out." Here, the continuous action (reading) was interrupted by the sudden event (lights going out).

💡 Key pattern: Past Continuous (ongoing action) + WHEN + Past Simple (sudden interruption).
Slide 10 Future — Overview

Now let's talk about the Future tense. In English, there isn't just one way to talk about the future — there are actually three main structures, and each one is used in a different context depending on how certain something is, and whether it was planned in advance or not.

The three forms are: Will, Be Going To, and the Present Continuous used for future. Let me explain each one.

Slide 11 Future — Auxiliaries: WILL / BE

Depending on the future structure, different auxiliaries are used. For WILL, the auxiliary is simply "will" — it never changes regardless of the subject. For Be Going To and Present Continuous, the auxiliary is the verb "to be" in its present forms: am, is, are.

"Will" does not change for any person — we say "I will, you will, he will, she will, they will." It's the same for everyone. In its contracted form, it becomes 'll: "I'll, she'll, they'll."

Slides 12–14 Future with WILL — Uses & Structure

WILL is used in three main situations. First, for spontaneous decisions — decisions made at the moment of speaking, not planned before. For example: someone asks "Who's going to answer the phone?" and you say "I'll get it!" — you decided just now.

Second, for predictions without evidence — when you believe something will happen but you have no proof. For example: "I think it will rain tomorrow." Third, for promises and offers: "I will help you with your homework." or "Don't worry, I won't tell anyone."

The structure is: Affirmative: Subject + will + base verb → "She will study." Negative: Subject + will not (won't) + base verb → "She won't study." Question: Will + Subject + base verb? → "Will she study?"

💡 WILL = unplanned / spontaneous decision or prediction with no evidence. If you thought about it before, use "going to" instead.
Slides 15–17 Future with BE GOING TO — Uses & Structure

Be Going To is used when the future action was planned or decided before the moment of speaking. There are three main uses. First, pre-planned intentions: "I'm going to visit my grandparents this weekend." — you decided this days ago. Second, predictions with evidence: you can see something is about to happen — "Look at those clouds, it's going to rain." Third, decisions already made: "She's going to study medicine." — this was already decided.

Structure: Affirmative: Subject + am/is/are + going to + base verb → "She is going to study." Negative: Subject + am/is/are + not + going to + base verb → "She isn't going to study." Question: Am/Is/Are + Subject + going to + base verb? → "Is she going to study?"

💡 "Going to" = already planned. You can see evidence. You already made the decision before speaking.
Slides 18–20 Future with Present Continuous — Uses & Structure

The third way to express the future in English is using the Present Continuous. This might seem confusing at first, but it's very common in everyday English. We use it to talk about fixed arrangements — plans that have already been confirmed, often involving other people or schedules.

Uses: Fixed future arrangements (confirmed plans), scheduled near-future events, and appointments and meetings. For example: "I'm meeting the doctor tomorrow at 10." — "They're flying to London next Friday." — "We're having dinner with my parents tonight."

Structure: Affirmative: Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing → "She is studying tomorrow." Negative: Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing → "She isn't studying tomorrow." Question: Am/Is/Are + Subject + verb-ing? → "Is she studying tomorrow?"

💡 Key difference: Present Continuous for future = confirmed, fixed arrangement (like a calendar entry). "Going to" = intention or plan but not necessarily locked in.
Slide 21 Closing — Thank You

So, to summarize what we've covered today: we looked at three verb tenses. The Past Simple, which tells us about completed actions in the past, using -ed for regular verbs or irregular forms, with DID as the auxiliary. The Past Continuous, which describes ongoing actions in the past, using was/were + verb-ing. And the Future, which has three forms: WILL for spontaneous decisions and predictions, BE GOING TO for plans and predictions with evidence, and the Present Continuous for confirmed arrangements.

Understanding these tenses and knowing when to use each one is key to communicating accurately and naturally in English. Thank you so much for watching — I hope this was helpful and clear. If you have any questions, feel free to ask!

Diapositiva 1 Introducción — Tiempos Verbales

¡Hola a todos! Hoy voy a hablar sobre los tiempos verbales en inglés. Los tiempos verbales son uno de los aspectos más importantes del idioma inglés porque nos permiten comunicar cuándo ocurre una acción — si sucedió en el pasado, está ocurriendo ahora mismo, o va a ocurrir en el futuro.

En esta presentación nos vamos a enfocar en tres grupos principales: el Past Simple (Pasado Simple), el Past Continuous (Pasado Continuo) y el Future (Futuro). Para cada uno voy a explicar qué es, cuándo se usa, cómo se forma y daré ejemplos. ¡Empecemos!

Diapositiva 2 Past Simple — Introducción

El primer tiempo que veremos es el Past Simple o Pasado Simple. Como su nombre lo indica, se usa para hablar de acciones que se completaron en el pasado — acciones que comenzaron y terminaron en un momento específico antes del presente.

Hay tres situaciones principales en las que usamos el Past Simple. Primero, para acciones completadas — cosas que ocurrieron y ya terminaron, como "I ate breakfast at seven o'clock" (Desayuné a las siete). Segundo, para hábitos del pasado — cosas que hacíamos regularmente, como "She walked to school every day when she was a child" (Ella caminaba al colegio todos los días cuando era niña). Y tercero, para eventos del pasado en secuencia — cuando narramos una historia y las acciones ocurren una después de otra.

Diapositiva 3 Past Simple — Verbo Auxiliar: DID

Ahora hablemos del verbo auxiliar del Past Simple. El auxiliar es DID, que proviene del verbo "to do" (hacer). Es importante saber que DID solo se usa en oraciones negativas e interrogativas — no en las afirmativas.

En oraciones negativas decimos "did not", o en su forma contraída, "didn't". Por ejemplo: "She didn't go to the party" (Ella no fue a la fiesta). En las preguntas, DID va al principio de la oración: "Did you call your mom?" (¿Llamaste a tu mamá?)

Una regla muy importante: cuando usas DID o DIDN'T, el verbo principal se queda en su forma base — NO cambia. Nunca se dice "He didn't went" — la forma correcta es "He didn't go".

💡 Truco: DID ya lleva el significado de pasado, por eso el verbo principal no necesita cambiar.
Diapositivas 4–5 Past Simple — Estructura y Formación

Veamos cómo se forma el Past Simple. Para las oraciones afirmativas, la estructura es: Sujeto + Verbo en Pasado + Objeto.

Aquí hay una distinción importante: los verbos en inglés son regulares o irregulares. Los verbos regulares simplemente agregan -ed al final: walk → walked, talk → talked, play → played, work → worked, visit → visited.

Los verbos irregulares, en cambio, cambian completamente y hay que memorizarlos: go → went, eat → ate, see → saw, buy → bought, write → wrote.

Para las oraciones negativas, la estructura es: Sujeto + did not (didn't) + verbo base + Objeto. Ejemplo: "Jim did not finish his homework" (Jim no terminó su tarea).

Para las preguntas, la estructura es: DID + Sujeto + verbo base + Objeto. Ejemplo: "Did you listen to music last night?" (¿Escuchaste música anoche?)

💡 Recuerda: en negativos y preguntas, el verbo principal SIEMPRE vuelve a su forma base porque DID ya marca el pasado.
Diapositiva 6 Past Continuous — Introducción

Ahora pasemos al Past Continuous o Pasado Continuo. Mientras el Past Simple describe una acción completada, el Past Continuous describe una acción que estaba en progreso en un momento específico del pasado — ya estaba ocurriendo y continuó por un período de tiempo.

Lo usamos en tres situaciones clave. Primero, para acciones que estaban en progreso en un momento específico del pasado. Segundo, para acciones interrumpidas — cuando una acción estaba en curso y otra acción la interrumpió de repente. Tercero, para acciones paralelas — dos acciones ocurriendo al mismo tiempo, generalmente unidas con la palabra "while" (mientras).

También lo usamos para establecer el contexto de fondo en una historia — describir qué estaba pasando cuando ocurrió el evento principal.

Diapositiva 7 Past Continuous — Auxiliar: WAS / WERE

Los verbos auxiliares del Past Continuous son WAS y WERE, que son las formas pasadas del verbo "to be" (ser/estar).

La elección entre WAS y WERE depende del sujeto: se usa WAS con sujetos singulares — I (yo), he (él), she (ella), it (ello). Se usa WERE con sujetos plurales — you (tú/ustedes), we (nosotros), they (ellos/ellas).

En oraciones negativas, usamos las formas contraídas: wasn't (was not) y weren't (were not). Por ejemplo: "He wasn't sleeping" o "They weren't paying attention".

💡 Truco para recordar: WAS = singular (1 persona), WERE = plural (más de 1) — y también "you", incluso cuando se refiere a una sola persona.
Diapositivas 8–9 Past Continuous — Estructura y Ejemplos

La estructura para las afirmativas es: Sujeto + was/were + verbo (-ing). El verbo principal siempre lleva la terminación -ing. Ejemplos: "She was studying at 9 pm last night" (Ella estaba estudiando a las 9 pm anoche). "He was cooking while she was watching TV" (Él estaba cocinando mientras ella veía televisión). "They were sleeping when the phone rang" (Estaban durmiendo cuando sonó el teléfono).

Para las negativas: Sujeto + was/were + not + verbo(-ing). Ejemplo: "She was not studying" o "She wasn't studying".

Para las preguntas: Was/Were + Sujeto + verbo(-ing)? Ejemplo: "Was she studying?" o "Were they listening?"

Un patrón muy común en inglés es combinar el Past Continuous con el Past Simple usando WHEN (cuando) o WHILE (mientras): "I was reading when the lights went out" (Estaba leyendo cuando se fueron las luces). La acción continua (leer) fue interrumpida por el evento repentino (se fueron las luces).

💡 Patrón clave: Past Continuous (acción en curso) + WHEN + Past Simple (interrupción repentina).
Diapositiva 10 Future — Introducción

Ahora hablemos del Future o Futuro. En inglés, no hay una sola forma de hablar del futuro — en realidad hay tres estructuras principales, y cada una se usa en un contexto diferente, dependiendo de qué tan seguro es algo y si fue planeado con anticipación o no.

Las tres formas son: Will, Be Going To y el Present Continuous usado para el futuro. Voy a explicar cada una.

Diapositiva 11 Future — Auxiliares: WILL / BE

Dependiendo de la estructura futura, se usan diferentes auxiliares. Para WILL, el auxiliar es simplemente "will" — nunca cambia sin importar el sujeto. Para Be Going To y el Present Continuous, el auxiliar es el verbo "to be" en sus formas presentes: am, is, are.

"Will" no cambia para ninguna persona — se dice "I will, you will, he will, she will, they will". En su forma contraída se convierte en 'll: "I'll, she'll, they'll".

Diapositivas 12–14 Futuro con WILL — Usos y Estructura

WILL se usa en tres situaciones principales. Primero, para decisiones espontáneas — decisiones tomadas en el momento de hablar, no planeadas antes. Por ejemplo: alguien pregunta "¿Quién va a contestar el teléfono?" y tú dices "I'll get it!" (¡Yo contesto!) — lo decidiste justo ahora.

Segundo, para predicciones sin evidencia — cuando crees que algo va a pasar pero no tienes prueba. Por ejemplo: "I think it will rain tomorrow" (Creo que va a llover mañana). Tercero, para promesas y ofrecimientos: "I will help you with your homework" (Te voy a ayudar con tu tarea) o "Don't worry, I won't tell anyone" (No te preocupes, no le voy a decir a nadie).

Estructura: Afirmativa: Sujeto + will + verbo base → "She will study." Negativa: Sujeto + will not (won't) + verbo base → "She won't study." Pregunta: Will + Sujeto + verbo base? → "Will she study?"

💡 WILL = decisión no planeada / espontánea, o predicción sin evidencia. Si ya lo habías pensado antes, usa "going to".
Diapositivas 15–17 Futuro con BE GOING TO — Usos y Estructura

Be Going To se usa cuando la acción futura fue planeada o decidida antes del momento de hablar. Hay tres usos principales. Primero, intenciones pre-planeadas: "I'm going to visit my grandparents this weekend" (Voy a visitar a mis abuelos este fin de semana) — lo decidiste hace días. Segundo, predicciones con evidencia: puedes ver que algo está a punto de pasar — "Look at those clouds, it's going to rain" (Mira esas nubes, va a llover). Tercero, decisiones ya tomadas: "She's going to study medicine" (Ella va a estudiar medicina) — eso ya estaba decidido.

Estructura: Afirmativa: Sujeto + am/is/are + going to + verbo base → "She is going to study." Negativa: Sujeto + am/is/are + not + going to + verbo base → "She isn't going to study." Pregunta: Am/Is/Are + Sujeto + going to + verbo base? → "Is she going to study?"

💡 "Going to" = ya planeado. Puedes ver evidencia. Ya tomaste la decisión antes de hablar.
Diapositivas 18–20 Futuro con Present Continuous — Usos y Estructura

La tercera forma de expresar el futuro en inglés es usando el Present Continuous. Esto puede parecer confuso al principio, pero es muy común en el inglés cotidiano. Lo usamos para hablar de arreglos fijos — planes que ya han sido confirmados, generalmente que involucran a otras personas o tienen una hora establecida.

Usos: Arreglos futuros fijos (planes confirmados), eventos programados en el futuro cercano y citas y reuniones. Por ejemplo: "I'm meeting the doctor tomorrow at 10" (Tengo cita con el médico mañana a las 10). "They're flying to London next Friday" (Ellos vuelan a Londres el próximo viernes). "We're having dinner with my parents tonight" (Cenamos con mis padres esta noche).

Estructura: Afirmativa: Sujeto + am/is/are + verbo-ing → "She is studying tomorrow." Negativa: Sujeto + am/is/are + not + verbo-ing → "She isn't studying tomorrow." Pregunta: Am/Is/Are + Sujeto + verbo-ing? → "Is she studying tomorrow?"

💡 Diferencia clave: Present Continuous para futuro = arreglo confirmado y fijo (como una entrada en el calendario). "Going to" = intención o plan, pero no necesariamente inamovible.
Diapositiva 21 Cierre — Thank You

Entonces, para resumir lo que vimos hoy: exploramos tres tiempos verbales. El Past Simple, que nos habla de acciones completadas en el pasado, usando -ed para verbos regulares o formas irregulares, con DID como auxiliar. El Past Continuous, que describe acciones en progreso en el pasado, usando was/were + verbo-ing. Y el Future, que tiene tres formas: WILL para decisiones espontáneas y predicciones, BE GOING TO para planes y predicciones con evidencia, y el Present Continuous para arreglos confirmados.

Entender estos tiempos y saber cuándo usar cada uno es clave para comunicarse de manera precisa y natural en inglés. ¡Muchas gracias por ver este video — espero que haya sido útil y claro. Si tienen alguna pregunta, no duden en hacerla!

⚡ Subject you → were. Question form: Were + subject + verb-ing? The word "while" shows two parallel past actions happening at the same time.

✅ were studying
⚡ Subject we → were. The phrase "At 10 PM last night" gives a specific past moment — a classic signal for Past Continuous (action in progress at that time).

✅ weren't playing
⚡ Negative Past Continuous: were + not + verb-ing → contracted: weren't playing. The contrast "they were working" confirms both are Past Continuous.

✅ was barking
⚡ Subject the dog → singular → was. Two parallel actions using "while". Both use Past Continuous to show they happened simultaneously.

✅ was watching
⚡ Subject she → singular → was. Classic pattern: Past Continuous (ongoing) + WHEN + Past Simple (interruption). She was in the middle of watching when the call came.
FUTURE (WILL / GOING TO / PRESENT CONTINUOUS) — 7 sentences
14
Look at those dark clouds! It ________ (rain) soon.
✅ is going to rain
⚡ There is visible evidence (dark clouds) → use Be Going To for predictions with evidence. "Will" would be wrong here because we have clear proof it's about to rain.
15
I think I ________ (pass) this Math term without any problems.
✅ will pass
⚡ The phrase "I think" signals a personal prediction without evidence → use WILL. This is an opinion about the future, not a planned intention.
16
We ________ (meet) at the main gate of the university at 2 PM tomorrow.
✅ are meeting
⚡ A fixed arrangement with a specific time and place → Present Continuous for future. This meeting is already confirmed and scheduled, like a calendar entry.
17
Next summer, I ________ (travel) to visit my family in another city.
✅ am going to travel
⚡ A pre-planned intention decided before speaking → Be Going To. The person already decided to travel; it's not spontaneous.
18
"The phone is ringing!" — "Don't worry, I ________ (answer) it."
✅ will answer
⚡ A spontaneous decision made at the exact moment of speaking → WILL. The person didn't plan this before; they decided right now, in response to the situation.
19
My team ________ (present) the final project in November.
✅ is presenting
⚡ A scheduled, confirmed event with a specific month → Present Continuous for future. This is a fixed date on the calendar, not just an intention.
20
I ________ (not / play) any games tonight; I need to rest.
✅ am not playing
⚡ A personal decision about a fixed plan for tonight → Present Continuous negative. The person has already decided their plan for the evening. Structure: am + not + verb-ing.
